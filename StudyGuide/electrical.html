Electrical Engineering

Electrical engineering reduces to analysis of charge behavior, field interactions, and system-level energy flow. The notes isolate each functional tier. Foundational principles define voltage, current, resistance, and power, establishing how energy moves through materials and components. Circuit theory provides the structural rules that govern interaction: series and parallel behavior, impedance, and the operational differences between passive and active devices. AC and DC distinctions determine waveform behavior, scaling, rectification, and filtering requirements.

Signal analysis exposes how systems behave in both time and frequency domains. It clarifies how components shape bandwidth, noise, distortion, and stability. Electronics extends these principles into amplifiers, switching devices, digital logic, and regulated power circuits, all tied to device physics and thermal constraints.

Electromagnetics delivers the field-level understanding that explains inductance, coupling, interference, and wave propagation. Control systems introduce feedback theory and mathematical modeling for stability and precision. Power systems scale the principles to grid-level structures: three-phase generation, long-distance transmission, protective devices, and grounding methods. The stack forms a unified discipline where physical laws govern every operational layer.
